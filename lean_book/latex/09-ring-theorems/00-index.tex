\chapter{Chapter 9: Ring examples and basic theorems}\label{sec:09-ring-theorems:00-index:1}

As in Chapter 7, the point here is the search process for each proof, not just the final term. Ring proofs are a good test of that process, because the goals become visually noisy fast (nested \texttt{addGrp.toGroup.op} everywhere). The skill under development is avoiding getting lost in that noise, and instead tracking which algebraic fact is one step away from use.

\textbf{Learning objectives.} By the end of this chapter, derive a ring fact that is \emph{not} an axiom (e.g.~\(a\cdot 0=0\)) from the axioms that are, spot when a ``mirror'' proof (\texttt{left\_\allowbreak{}distrib} vs.~\texttt{right\_\allowbreak{}distrib}) can be copied line-by-line rather than re-derived, and reduce a ``compute this'' goal to a ``verify this satisfies the characterizing property'' goal via a previously-proved uniqueness lemma.
